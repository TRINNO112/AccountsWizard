üë§ 1. Sole Proprietorship (Ekaki Vyapaar)

üìñ Definition

Sole Proprietorship wo business form hai jisme ek hi vyakti business ka owner hota hai. Wo akela hi saari decisions leta hai, saara profit apne paas rakhta hai, aur saare losses bhi akela hi bear karta hai. Ye sabse purana aur simple form hai business ka.

üîπ Single Ownership
Business ka ek hi malik hota hai jo saara investment karta hai
üîπ No Separate Legal Entity
Owner aur business alag nahi hote - dono ek hi maane jaate hain
üîπ Unlimited Liability
Agar business mein loss ho toh owner ki personal property bhi use ho sakti hai debts pay karne ke liye
üîπ No Legal Formalities
Koi special registration ya legal procedure nahi chahiye (except specific trades)

‚úÖ Advantages (Fayde)

  • Easy formation - Bahut aasaan start karna
  • Full control - Saare decisions khud le sakte ho
  • All profits - Poora profit khud rakh sakte ho
  • Secrecy - Business secrets safe
  • Quick decisions - Kisi se puchne ki zaroorat nahi
  • Direct motivation - Mehnat ka poora result khud ko milta hai
  • Personal touch - Customers se direct relation
  • Flexibility - Easily change kar sakte ho business

‚ùå Disadvantages (Nuksan)

  • Unlimited liability - Personal property risk mein
  • Limited capital - Sirf apne paise se hi kaam
  • Limited skills - Ek insaan sab kuch nahi jaanta
  • Uncertain life - Owner ke saath business bhi khatam
  • No economies of scale - Large scale benefits nahi
  • Overburden - Saara kaam akele karna
  • Limited growth - Expansion difficult hai

üìç Examples of Sole Proprietorship:

‚Ä¢ Retail Shops: Kirana store, Medical store, Stationery shop
‚Ä¢ Service Providers: Tailors, Beauticians, Tutors
‚Ä¢ Professionals: Doctors, Lawyers, CAs (practicing individually)
‚Ä¢ Small Manufacturers: Cottage industries, Handicrafts
‚Ä¢ Food Business: Small restaurants, Food stalls

‚ö†Ô∏è Suitability (Kab suitable hai?):

‚úì Jab capital requirement kam ho
‚úì Jab personal attention zaroori ho
‚úì Jab quick decisions lene ho
‚úì Small scale operations ke liye
‚úì Jab secrecy important ho

üè† 3. Hindu Undivided Family (HUF) Business

üìñ Definition

HUF Business wo business hai jo Hindu family ke ancestral property se chalta hai. Ye sirf Hindu, Jain, Sikh aur Buddhist families ke liye applicable hai.

üîπ Karta
Family ka sabse senior male member jo business manage karta hai. Uski liability unlimited hoti hai.
üîπ Coparceners
Karta + uske sons, grandsons, great-grandsons. Unki liability limited hoti hai (ancestral property tak).
üîπ Ancestral Property
Wo property jo 4 generations se family mein chal rahi hai

‚úÖ Features of HUF Business

  • Governed by Hindu Law
  • Membership by birth
  • Karta has full control
  • Minor can also be member
  • Three generations can be coparceners
  • Female members are now coparceners (after 2005 amendment)

üìã Rights of Coparceners

  • Right to joint possession
  • Right to partition
  • Right to accounts
  • Right to restrain Karta from misusing property
  • Right to maintenance

üè™ 5. Cooperative Society

üìñ Definition

Cooperative Society ek voluntary association hai jisme log apni common economic needs ko fulfill karne ke liye milkar kaam karte hain. Ye "all for each and each for all" principle par kaam karti hai.

üåü Core Principles of Cooperation

1. Voluntary Membership

Koi bhi join kar sakta hai, koi bhi chhod sakta hai

2. Democratic Control

One member = One vote, chahe kitna bhi share ho

3. Limited Return on Capital

Dividend limited hota hai (usually 9-12%)

4. Distribution of Surplus

Profits members mein patronage basis par divide

Types of Cooperative Societies:

üõí Consumer Cooperative

  • Consumers ke liye goods buy karna
  • Middlemen eliminate karna
  • Fair prices provide karna
  • Example: Kendriya Bhandar, Apna Bazar

üè≠ Producer Cooperative

  • Small producers ka association
  • Raw materials together buy karna
  • Collectively produce karna
  • Example: Handloom cooperatives

üí∞ Credit Cooperative

  • Members ko loans dena
  • Savings encourage karna
  • Low interest rates
  • Example: Village credit societies

üè† Housing Cooperative

  • Members ke liye houses banana
  • Land acquire karna collectively
  • Construction karwana
  • Example: DDA housing

üåæ Marketing Cooperative

  • Farmers ka produce sell karna
  • Fair prices dilwana
  • Example: AMUL, Mother Dairy

üöú Farming Cooperative

  • Small farmers ka land pool karna
  • Joint cultivation
  • Sharing of equipment

‚úÖ Advantages of Cooperative Society

  • Easy formation - min 10 members
  • Limited liability
  • Democratic management
  • Stable existence
  • Government support
  • Tax benefits
  • Service motive (not profit)

‚ùå Disadvantages of Cooperative Society

  • Limited capital
  • Inefficient management
  • Lack of secrecy
  • Government interference
  • Conflicts among members
  • Lack of motivation
  • Corruption possible

üìä Registration Requirements:

‚Ä¢ Minimum 10 members required for registration
‚Ä¢ Registered under Cooperative Societies Act, 1912 or State Acts
‚Ä¢ Registrar of Cooperative Societies ke paas register hoti hai

Principles of Insurance:

1. Utmost Good Faith (Uberrimae Fidei)
Dono parties ko poora sach batana chahiye. Koi bhi material fact chhupana nahi chahiye.
2. Insurable Interest
Insured ko subject matter mein financial interest hona chahiye. Loss se directly affect hona chahiye.
3. Indemnity
Insurance sirf actual loss ka compensation deta hai, profit nahi. Insured original position mein wapas aana chahiye.

Claim pay karne ke baad, insurer ko insured ki jagah lene ka right milta hai against third party.
5. Contribution
Agar same subject ke liye multiple insurance ho, toh sab insurers milkar proportionately pay karenge.
6. Proximate Cause (Causa Proxima)
Claim tab milega jab loss ka immediate/nearest cause insured peril ho.
7. Mitigation of Loss
Insured ko loss minimize karne ki koshish karni chahiye.

General Insurance: Property/assets insure hote hain. Indemnity principle apply hota hai (actual loss ka compensation).

üå± Chapter 6: Social Responsibility of Business & Business Ethics

üìå 1. Concept of Social Responsibility

Meaning: Business ek social institution hai. Ye society ke resources (Money, Men, Material, Machine) use karta hai. Isliye, business ki ye moral duty banti hai ki wo sirf profit na kamaye, balki society ke welfare ke liye bhi kaam kare.

Social responsibility ka matlab hai wo decisions lena aur wo actions perform karna jo society ke objectives aur values ke liye desirable hain.

üí° The Reality: Why Business MUST be Responsible?

  • Threat of Public Regulation: Agar business socially responsible nahi hoga, toh Government strict laws banayegi (e.g., Pollution control laws).
  • Pressure of Labour Movement: Aajkal workers educated hain. Agar unhe exploit kiya gaya toh wo strike kar sakte hain.
  • Impact of Consumer Consciousness: Consumers aware hain. Wo unethical companies ke products boycott kar sakte hain.
  • Social License to Operate: Society allow karti hai business ko operate karna. Agar society naraz ho gayi, toh business survive nahi kar payega.

üèóÔ∏è 2. Kinds of Social Responsibility

Social responsibility ko 4 main categories mein divide kiya ja sakta hai:

1. Economic Responsibility
Ye sabse basic responsibility hai. Business ka primary kaam hai Goods Produce karna aur Profit Kamana. Without profit, business survive nahi kar sakta.
2. Legal Responsibility
Business ko desh ke Laws aur Regulations follow karne chahiye. Tax pay karna, pollution norms maanna‚Äîye sab legal responsibility hai. Legal rehna socially responsible business ki pehchan hai.
3. Ethical Responsibility
Ye laws ke aage ki baat hai. Wo behaviour jo society expect karti hai lekin law mein likha nahi hai. Example: Fair trade practices, workers ki dignity maintain karna, truthful advertising.
4. Discretionary (Philanthropic) Responsibility
Ye puri tarah voluntary hai (Apni marzi se). Example: Schools banana, hospitals kholna, floods/earthquakes mein donation dena. Agar business ye nahi karega toh koi case nahi hoga, par karega toh goodwill badhegi.

‚öñÔ∏è 3. The Great Debate: Arguments For & Against

‚úÖ Arguments FOR Social Responsibility (Kyun karna chahiye?)

  1. Justification for Existence and Growth: Business society ki needs puri karne ke liye bana hai. Agar wo society ki care karega, tabhi grow karega.
  2. Long-term Interest of the Firm: Short term mein shayad kharcha ho, lekin long term mein image achi banti hai jisse sales aur profit badhta hai.
  3. Avoidance of Government Regulation: Voluntary social work karne se government interference kam ho jata hai.
  4. Maintenance of Society: Agar society mein poverty aur crime hoga toh business safe nahi rahega. Society ko better banana business ki safety ke liye zaroori hai.
  5. Availability of Resources with Business: Business ke paas Money aur Talented Managers hote hain jo social problems solve kar sakte hain.
  6. Converting Problems into Opportunities: Example: Waste recycling business environment bhi bachaata hai aur profit bhi deta hai.

‚ùå Arguments AGAINST Social Responsibility (Kyun nahi karna chahiye?)

  1. Violation of Profit Maximization: Milton Friedman ne kaha tha "The only responsibility of business is to maximize profit." Social work kharcha badhata hai aur profit kam karta hai.
  2. Burden on Consumers: CSR ka kharcha business apni jeb se nahi deta, wo prices badha kar consumers se hi vasool karta hai.
  3. Lack of Social Skills: Businessmen profit kamane mein expert hote hain, social problems solve karne ki training unke paas nahi hoti.
  4. Lack of Broad Public Support: Public business ki interference social matters mein pasand nahi karti.

üë• 4. Social Responsibility Towards Different Interest Groups

Stakeholders wo log hain jo business se directly ya indirectly jude hue hain.

A. Towards Shareholders (Owners/Investors)
  • Fair Return on Investment (Dividend) ensure karna.
  • Unke capital (paisa) ki safety ensure karna.
  • Company ki financial position ki sahi information dena.
  • Company ki goodwill aur reputation badhana.
B. Towards Workers (Employees)
  • Fair wages aur salary time par dena.
  • Safe working conditions provide karna (e.g., ventilation, water).
  • Promotion aur career growth ki opportunities dena.
  • Workers ke democratic rights (Union banane ka right) respect karna.
C. Towards Consumers
  • Right Quality aur Quantity ke goods supply karna.
  • Reasonable prices charge karna.
  • Adulteration (milawat) aur hoarding (jama khori) nahi karna.
  • Honest advertising (Jhoothe waade nahi).
  • Customer grievances (shikayatein) ko handle karna.
D. Towards Government
  • Taxes honestly aur time par pay karna.
  • Rules, laws aur regulations follow karna.
  • Corrupt practices (bribing officials) avoid karna.
  • National problems (unemployment, poverty) solve karne mein help karna.
E. Towards Community/Society
  • Environment protect karna (Pollution control).
  • Jobs create karna.
  • Weaker sections ki help karna.
  • Culture aur heritage ko preserve karna.

üå≥ 5. Business and Environmental Protection

Pollution environment ke physical, chemical aur biological characteristics mein harmful change hai. Business production karta hai jisse waste generate hota hai, isliye environment protection business ki badi zimmedari hai.

Types of Pollution

  • Air Pollution: Carbon monoxide aur smoke jo factories aur vehicles se nikalta hai. Causes global warming and lung diseases.
  • Water Pollution: Industrial waste aur chemicals ko river mein dump karna. Kills aquatic life and makes water undrinkable.
  • Land Pollution: Toxic waste ko zameen par fekna jisse soil fertility khatam ho jati hai.
  • Noise Pollution: Generators aur machines ka shor. Causes hearing loss and mental stress.

Need for Pollution Control (Why save environment?)

  1. Reduction of Health Hazards: Cancer, heart attack, aur lung diseases kam karne ke liye.
  2. Reduced Risk of Liability: Agar business pollution karega, toh log uspe case kar denge aur bhari compensation dena padega.
  3. Cost Savings: Waste management aur better technology se raw material kam lagta hai aur cost bachti hai.
  4. Improved Public Image: Environment-friendly companies ko society respect karti hai.
  5. Other Social Benefits: Clear visibility, clean buildings, better quality of life.

Role of Business in Environmental Protection

  • Top Management Commitment: Bosses ko committed hona padega.
  • Clear Policies: Written policy honi chahiye environment save karne ke liye.
  • Following Laws: Govt ke pollution norms strictly follow karna.
  • Clean Technology: Aisi machines use karna jo kam pollution karein.
  • Education: Employees aur suppliers ko educate karna.

‚öñÔ∏è 6. Business Ethics

üìñ Definition

Ethics word Greek word 'Ethos' se aaya hai, meaning 'Character'.
Business Ethics refer to socially determined moral principles which govern business activities. Ye define karta hai ki kya "Right" hai aur kya "Wrong" hai.

Examples of Unethical Practices

  • Adulteration (Khane mein milawat).
  • Black Marketing & Hoarding.
  • Misleading Advertisements.
  • Exploiting Workers (Kam salary, unsafe environment).
  • Bribing Government officials.

Elements of Business Ethics

  1. Top Management Commitment: CEO aur higher managers ko role model banna padega. Agar wo ethical honge, toh puri company ethical hogi.
  2. Publication of a 'Code': Ek written document ('Code of Conduct') hona chahiye jo bataye ki honesty, quality aur safety ke standards kya hain.
  3. Establishment of Compliance Mechanisms: Check karne ka system hona chahiye ki log ethics follow kar rahe hain ya nahi.
  4. Involving Employees at All Levels: Ethics policies banate waqt employees ki rai leni chahiye.
  5. Measuring Results: Regular audit karna chahiye ki ethical standards kitne follow ho rahe hain.

üí∞ Chapter 7: Sources of Business Finance

üìå 1. Meaning & Nature of Business Finance

Definition: Business Finance refers to the money required for carrying out business activities. Just as blood is necessary for human life, finance is the "life blood" of business. It is needed to establish a business, run it, modernize it, and expand it.

üí° Why does a Business need Money? (Financial Needs)

Financial requirements can be categorized into two main types:

A. Fixed Capital Requirements

This is money needed to purchase fixed assets like land, buildings, plant, and machinery.

  • Time Horizon: Long-term (more than 5 years).
  • Nature: This capital gets blocked in the business for a long time.
  • Factors: A manufacturing firm needs more fixed capital than a trading firm.
B. Working Capital Requirements

This is money needed for day-to-day operations like buying raw materials, paying wages, salaries, rent, and taxes.

  • Time Horizon: Short-term (usually less than 1 year).
  • Nature: This capital circulates in the business (Cash → Raw Material → Goods → Sales → Cash).

üìä 2. Classification of Financial Sources

To choose the right source, we classify them based on Time, Ownership, and Source of Generation.

‚è≥ 1. On the Basis of Period

  • Long Term (> 5 Years): Required for fixed assets.
    Ex: Equity Shares, Debentures, Retained Earnings, Loans from Financial Institutions.
  • Medium Term (1 to 5 Years): Required for modernization or ads.
    Ex: Public Deposits, Lease Financing, Loans from Commercial Banks.
  • Short Term (< 1 Year): Required for working capital.
    Ex: Trade Credit, Factoring, Commercial Paper, Bank Overdraft.

üë§ 2. On the Basis of Ownership

  • Owner's Funds: Capital provided by the owners. It is "Risk Capital" and stays permanently.
    Ex: Equity Shares, Preference Shares, Retained Earnings.
  • Borrowed Funds: Capital borrowed from outsiders. It creates a liability and requires interest payment.
    Ex: Debentures, Loans, Public Deposits.

üîÑ 3. On the Basis of Generation

  • Internal Sources: Funds generated inside the business.
    Ex: Retained Earnings (Ploughing back of profits), Selling surplus assets.
  • External Sources: Funds raised from outside the business.
    Ex: Issue of Shares, Debentures, Bank Loans.

üè† 3. Owner's Funds (Detailed Analysis)

A. Equity Shares

Equity shares represent the ownership of a company. They are the primary risk bearers. They do not have a fixed dividend; they get paid only after everyone else has been paid.

‚úÖ Merits (Advantages)

  • Permanent Capital: It is a permanent source of finance and does not need to be refunded during the life of the company.
  • No Fixed Burden: The company is not legally compelled to pay dividends if there is no profit.
  • Asset Safety: No assets need to be mortgaged (pledged) as security to issue shares.
  • Voting Rights: Equity shareholders get the right to vote and participate in management.

‚ùå Demerits (Disadvantages)

  • Risk of Fluctuating Return: Investors get irregular income. If profit is low, dividend is low or zero.
  • Dilution of Control: Issuing more shares brings in new owners, reducing the control of existing shareholders.
  • High Cost: Flotation cost (cost of issuing shares like advertising, underwriting) is higher than other sources.
  • Complex Procedure: There are many legal formalities and SEBI guidelines to follow.

B. Preference Shares

These are a "Hybrid" security. They are shares, but they act a bit like debt because they offer a fixed rate of return. They have two preferential rights over equity shares: (1) Receiving dividend first, (2) Receiving capital back first during winding up.

‚úÖ Merits (Advantages)

  • Steady Income: Suitable for investors who want fixed earnings with low risk.
  • No Dilution of Control: Preference shareholders usually do not have voting rights, so management control remains safe.
  • Flexibility: The company can issue redeemable preference shares which can be paid back when funds are not needed.

‚ùå Demerits (Disadvantages)

  • Not Tax Deductible: The dividend paid is not treated as an expense, so no tax saving for the company.
  • Risk for Company: Even in low profit years, dividend accumulates (in cumulative shares), creating a burden.
  • Low Returns in Boom: Even if the company earns huge profits, preference shareholders get only the fixed rate.

C. Retained Earnings

A portion of the net profit which is not distributed as dividends but is saved for future use. It is also called "Ploughing Back of Profits" or "Self-Financing".

  • ‚úÖ Merit - Most Economical: It has no explicit cost (no interest, no flotation cost).
  • ‚úÖ Merit - Operational Freedom: Since it is the company's own money, there are no restrictions on how to use it.
  • ‚ùå Demerit - Dissatisfaction: Excessive ploughing back may cause dissatisfaction among shareholders as they get lower dividends.
  • ‚ùå Demerit - Uncertain: It depends entirely on profits. No profit means no retained earnings.

üè¶ 4. Borrowed Funds (Detailed Analysis)

A. Debentures

A debenture is an acknowledgement of debt issued by a company under its common seal. It carries a fixed rate of interest.

‚úÖ Merits

  • Low Risk for Investors: Interest is paid whether the company makes a profit or loss.
  • Tax Shield (Crucial): Interest paid on debentures is tax-deductible. It lowers the company's tax bill.
  • No Control Dilution: Debenture holders are creditors, not owners. They have no voting rights.

‚ùå Demerits

  • Fixed Obligation: It creates a permanent burden of interest payment, which is risky during loss periods.
  • Charge on Assets: Usually, companies have to mortgage assets to issue debentures. This restricts the use of assets.

B. Loans from Financial Institutions (FIs)

Institutions like LIC, SIDBI, IFCI are established by the government to provide long-term finance and technical advice to industries.

  • ‚úÖ Long Term Finance: Best source for expansion and modernization projects.
  • ‚úÖ Managerial Advice: They provide technical and consultancy services too.
  • ‚ùå Interference: They often appoint their nominee on the Board of Directors, restricting the company's freedom.
  • ‚ùå Strict Procedure: The process of getting a loan is very lengthy and involves strict scrutiny.

C. Public Deposits

The company invites the general public to deposit money with it for a fixed period (6 months to 3 years) at a higher interest rate than banks.

  • ‚úÖ Lower Cost: For the company, it is cheaper than a bank loan. For the public, it gives higher interest than bank FDs.
  • ‚úÖ No Security: These are unsecured deposits, so company assets are free.
  • ‚ùå Unreliable: It is "Fair Weather Friend". The public may not deposit money when the company is in trouble.

D. Trade Credit

It refers to the credit extended by one trader to another for the purchase of goods and services. It facilitates the purchase of supplies without immediate payment.

  • ‚úÖ Convenient: It is a continuous and spontaneous source of finance. No special agreement is needed.
  • ‚úÖ Readily Available: If the company has a good reputation, suppliers easily give credit.
  • ‚ùå High Cost: Often, suppliers increase the price of goods to cover the credit risk.

üÜï 5. Specialized & Modern Sources

Commercial Paper (CP)

Concept: An unsecured promissory note issued by large, highly creditworthy companies to raise short-term funds (15 days to 1 year). Usually used for seasonal working capital or bridge financing.

Status: It is an instrument of the money market and is sold at a discount and redeemed at par.

Lease Financing

Concept: A contractual agreement where the owner of an asset (Lessor) grants the right to use the asset to another party (Lessee) in return for periodic payments (Lease Rentals).

Merit: The lessee can use expensive machinery without investing a huge amount of capital to buy it.

Factoring

Concept: A financial service where a business sells its "Accounts Receivables" (Bills/Debtors) to a third party (Factor) at a discount.

Function: The Factor gives immediate cash (advance) and takes the responsibility of collecting money from the debtors.

Inter-Corporate Deposits (ICD)

Concept: An unsecured short-term loan extended by one corporate to another. It is very popular for solving immediate cash shortages.

Risk: High risk, high interest rate.

üåç 6. International Sources of Finance

In the era of globalization, Indian companies can raise funds from international markets using these instruments:

1. GDR (Global Depository Receipt)

What is it? A receipt issued by an overseas depository bank against shares of an Indian company.

  • Market: Can be listed on any foreign stock exchange (e.g., London, Luxembourg) except the USA.
  • Currency: Denominated in US Dollars.
  • Voting: Holders do not have voting rights.

2. ADR (American Depository Receipt)

What is it? Similar to GDR, but strictly for the American market.

  • Market: Can be issued only to American citizens and listed on US exchanges (NYSE, NASDAQ).
  • Regulations: Subject to strict disclosure rules of the SEC (Securities and Exchange Commission) of USA.

3. IDR (Indian Depository Receipt)

What is it? The reverse of GDR.

  • Concept: A financial instrument that allows Foreign Companies to raise funds from the Indian Market.
  • Example: Standard Chartered Bank was the first to issue IDR in India.

ü§î 7. Which Source to Choose? (Factors)

A business cannot rely on just one source. The choice depends on a combination of these factors:

  • üí∞ Cost: The cost of raising funds (flotation cost) and the cost of utilizing funds (interest/dividend) should be minimal.
  • üìâ Financial Strength: A company with stable earnings can afford Debt (Debentures). A company with unstable earnings should stick to Equity.
  • ‚ö†Ô∏è Risk Profile: Debt is risky because interest is a fixed burden. If the company is already taking high business risks, it should avoid financial risk (Debt).
  • üéÆ Control: If existing shareholders want to retain full control, they should avoid issuing more Equity Shares and prefer Debt.
  • üìÖ Time Period: For less than 1 year, go for Trade Credit or Bank Overdraft. For 20 years, go for Equity.
