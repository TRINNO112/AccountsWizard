üë§ 1. Sole Proprietorship (Ekaki Vyapaar)

üìñ Definition

Sole Proprietorship wo business form hai jisme ek hi vyakti business ka owner hota hai. Wo akela hi saari decisions leta hai, saara profit apne paas rakhta hai, aur saare losses bhi akela hi bear karta hai. Ye sabse purana aur simple form hai business ka.

üîπ Single Ownership
Business ka ek hi malik hota hai jo saara investment karta hai
üîπ No Separate Legal Entity
Owner aur business alag nahi hote - dono ek hi maane jaate hain
üîπ Unlimited Liability
Agar business mein loss ho toh owner ki personal property bhi use ho sakti hai debts pay karne ke liye
üîπ No Legal Formalities
Koi special registration ya legal procedure nahi chahiye (except specific trades)

‚úÖ Advantages (Fayde)

  • Easy formation - Bahut aasaan start karna
  • Full control - Saare decisions khud le sakte ho
  • All profits - Poora profit khud rakh sakte ho
  • Secrecy - Business secrets safe
  • Quick decisions - Kisi se puchne ki zaroorat nahi
  • Direct motivation - Mehnat ka poora result khud ko milta hai
  • Personal touch - Customers se direct relation
  • Flexibility - Easily change kar sakte ho business

‚ùå Disadvantages (Nuksan)

  • Unlimited liability - Personal property risk mein
  • Limited capital - Sirf apne paise se hi kaam
  • Limited skills - Ek insaan sab kuch nahi jaanta
  • Uncertain life - Owner ke saath business bhi khatam
  • No economies of scale - Large scale benefits nahi
  • Overburden - Saara kaam akele karna
  • Limited growth - Expansion difficult hai

üìç Examples of Sole Proprietorship:

‚Ä¢ Retail Shops: Kirana store, Medical store, Stationery shop
‚Ä¢ Service Providers: Tailors, Beauticians, Tutors
‚Ä¢ Professionals: Doctors, Lawyers, CAs (practicing individually)
‚Ä¢ Small Manufacturers: Cottage industries, Handicrafts
‚Ä¢ Food Business: Small restaurants, Food stalls

üè† 3. Hindu Undivided Family (HUF) Business

üìñ Definition

HUF Business wo business hai jo Hindu family ke ancestral property se chalta hai. Ye sirf Hindu, Jain, Sikh aur Buddhist families ke liye applicable hai.

üîπ Karta
Family ka sabse senior male member jo business manage karta hai. Uski liability unlimited hoti hai.
üîπ Coparceners
Karta + uske sons, grandsons, great-grandsons. Unki liability limited hoti hai (ancestral property tak).
üîπ Ancestral Property
Wo property jo 4 generations se family mein chal rahi hai

‚úÖ Features of HUF Business

  • Governed by Hindu Law
  • Membership by birth
  • Karta has full control
  • Minor can also be member
  • Three generations can be coparceners
  • Female members are now coparceners (after 2005 amendment)

üìã Rights of Coparceners

  • Right to joint possession
  • Right to partition
  • Right to accounts
  • Right to restrain Karta from misusing property
  • Right to maintenance

üè™ 5. Cooperative Society

üìñ Definition

Cooperative Society ek voluntary association hai jisme log apni common economic needs ko fulfill karne ke liye milkar kaam karte hain. Ye "all for each and each for all" principle par kaam karti hai.

üåü Core Principles of Cooperation

1. Voluntary Membership

Koi bhi join kar sakta hai, koi bhi chhod sakta hai

2. Democratic Control

One member = One vote, chahe kitna bhi share ho

3. Limited Return on Capital

Dividend limited hota hai (usually 9-12%)

4. Distribution of Surplus

Profits members mein patronage basis par divide

Types of Cooperative Societies:

üõí Consumer Cooperative

  • Consumers ke liye goods buy karna
  • Middlemen eliminate karna
  • Fair prices provide karna
  • Example: Kendriya Bhandar, Apna Bazar

üè≠ Producer Cooperative

  • Small producers ka association
  • Raw materials together buy karna
  • Collectively produce karna
  • Example: Handloom cooperatives

üí∞ Credit Cooperative

  • Members ko loans dena
  • Savings encourage karna
  • Low interest rates
  • Example: Village credit societies

üè† Housing Cooperative

  • Members ke liye houses banana
  • Land acquire karna collectively
  • Construction karwana
  • Example: DDA housing

üåæ Marketing Cooperative

  • Farmers ka produce sell karna
  • Fair prices dilwana
  • Example: AMUL, Mother Dairy

üöú Farming Cooperative

  • Small farmers ka land pool karna
  • Joint cultivation
  • Sharing of equipment

‚úÖ Advantages of Cooperative Society

  • Easy formation - min 10 members
  • Limited liability
  • Democratic management
  • Stable existence
  • Government support
  • Tax benefits
  • Service motive (not profit)

‚ùå Disadvantages of Cooperative Society

  • Limited capital
  • Inefficient management
  • Lack of secrecy
  • Government interference
  • Conflicts among members
  • Lack of motivation
  • Corruption possible

üìä Registration Requirements:

‚Ä¢ Minimum 10 members required for registration
‚Ä¢ Registered under Cooperative Societies Act, 1912 or State Acts
‚Ä¢ Registrar of Cooperative Societies ke paas register hoti hai

Principles of Insurance (Detailed):

1. Utmost Good Faith (Uberrimae Fidei)
Both parties must disclose all material facts honestly.
Example: If you have a heart disease, you must tell the life insurer. Hiding it can void the policy.
Applies to: All types of insurance
2. Insurable Interest
You must have financial interest in the subject matter. You should suffer financially if loss occurs.
Example: You can insure your own car, not your neighbor's car.
Life Insurance: Interest at time of contract
Fire Insurance: Interest at contract AND loss
Marine Insurance: Interest at time of loss
3. Indemnity
Insurance compensates only actual loss, not profit. Insured should be restored to original position.
Example: If car worth ‚Çπ5 lakh is damaged and repair costs ‚Çπ2 lakh, you get ‚Çπ2 lakh (not ‚Çπ5 lakh).
Exception: Life insurance - fixed sum assured paid (no indemnity)
4. Subrogation
After paying claim, insurer gets right to sue the third party who caused the loss.
Example: Your car is damaged by someone else. Insurer pays you, then sues that person to recover money.
Applies to: General insurance only (not life insurance)
5. Contribution
If same subject is insured with multiple insurers, all will share the claim proportionately.
Example: House insured for ‚Çπ50L with Company A and ‚Çπ50L with Company B. Total ‚Çπ1 crore insured, but actual value ‚Çπ80L. On loss of ‚Çπ40L, each pays ‚Çπ20L.
Purpose: Prevent profit from insurance
6. Proximate Cause (Causa Proxima)
Claim is paid only if the nearest/immediate cause of loss is an insured peril.
Example: Fire insurance ‚Üí Fire caused by earthquake ‚Üí If earthquake not covered, no claim even though fire occurred.
7. Mitigation of Loss
Insured must take reasonable steps to minimize the loss.
Example: If fire breaks out, you should try to extinguish it, call fire brigade, not just watch and claim later.

üå± Chapter 6: Social Responsibility of Business & Business Ethics

üìå Introduction

Business sirf profit kamane ke liye nahi hai. Business society ka ek important part hai - ye society se resources leta hai (land, labor, capital, customers) aur society mein operate karta hai. Isliye business ki bhi society ke towards kuch responsibilities hain. Ye concept hai Social Responsibility ka.

üìñ What is Social Responsibility?

Social Responsibility of Business means the obligation of business to act in a manner which is beneficial to society at large. It goes beyond just making profits - business should consider the impact of its actions on all stakeholders including employees, customers, community, environment, and society.

Why Should Business be Socially Responsible?

  • ‚Ä¢ Business uses society's resources (land, people, infrastructure)
  • ‚Ä¢ Business exists and operates within society
  • ‚Ä¢ Business earns from society (customers are from society)
  • ‚Ä¢ Business has power and influence - should use it responsibly
  • ‚Ä¢ Long-term survival depends on society's well-being
  • ‚Ä¢ "What is good for society is good for business in the long run"

ü§ù 1. Concept of Social Responsibility

üìä Evolution of Business Attitude

Era Business Attitude
Traditional View "Business of business is business" - Only profit matters
Modern View "Business of business is also society" - Profit + Social welfare

Arguments FOR Social Responsibility:

‚úÖ Why Business SHOULD be Socially Responsible

  • 1. Justification for Existence: Business exists because society allows it. Should give back.
  • 2. Long-term Self-Interest: Healthy society = healthy business environment. Better customers, employees.
  • 3. Avoid Government Regulation: If business acts responsibly, less need for strict laws.
  • 4. Resources from Society: Business uses society's resources, should return something.
  • 5. Business Has Power: With great power comes great responsibility.
  • 6. Better Public Image: Socially responsible companies have better reputation.
  • 7. Moral Obligation: It's simply the right thing to do.
  • 8. Business Can Solve Problems: Has resources and expertise to help society.
  • 9. Stakeholder Satisfaction: Keeps all stakeholders happy, not just shareholders.
  • 10. Sustainable Development: Ensures resources for future generations.

Arguments AGAINST Social Responsibility:

‚ùå Why Some Say Business Should Focus Only on Profit

  • 1. Primary Purpose is Profit: Milton Friedman - "The business of business is business"
  • 2. Lack of Social Skills: Businessmen trained for profit, not social work.
  • 3. Increased Costs: Social activities add to costs, reduce profits.
  • 4. Against Profit Maximization: Resources diverted from core business.
  • 5. Accountability Issue: Managers accountable to shareholders, not society.
  • 6. Already Pays Taxes: Government should handle social issues with tax money.
  • 7. Too Much Power: Would give business even more power over society.
  • 8. Conflicting Interests: Different stakeholders want different things.

üí° Balanced View

Modern approach says business should pursue PROFIT WITH RESPONSIBILITY. Profit is necessary for survival, but not at the cost of society. Business should find ways to be profitable while also being socially responsible. This is the concept of "Shared Value" - creating economic value in a way that also creates value for society.

üè† Owner's Funds (Ownership Capital)

üìñ What are Owner's Funds?

Owner's Funds are the funds that belong to the owners of the company. These funds are not required to be repaid. Owners take the maximum risk and get returns only after all other obligations are met. Owner's funds include Equity Share Capital, Preference Share Capital, and Retained Earnings.

1. Equity Shares (Ordinary Shares / Common Stock)

üìñ Definition

Equity shares represent ownership in a company. Equity shareholders are the real owners of the company. They have voting rights, participate in management decisions, and share in the profits (dividends) and losses of the company.

üîπ Ownership
Equity shareholders are the real owners of the company. They own a portion of the company based on their shareholding.
üîπ Voting Rights
Each equity share carries one vote. Shareholders vote on important matters like appointment of directors, mergers, etc.
üîπ Variable Dividend
Dividend is not fixed. It depends on profits and board's decision. In loss, no dividend may be paid.
üîπ Residual Claim
At the time of winding up, equity shareholders are paid LAST - after all creditors, debenture holders, and preference shareholders.
üîπ Permanent Capital
Equity capital is not repayable during the lifetime of the company. It is permanent source of funds.
üîπ Transferable
Equity shares can be transferred/sold to others (in case of public company, through stock exchange).
üîπ Pre-emptive Right
Existing shareholders have first right to buy new shares issued by company (Rights Issue).
üîπ Limited Liability
Shareholders' liability is limited to the amount unpaid on their shares. Personal assets are safe.

‚úÖ Advantages of Equity Shares

  • No Fixed Obligation: Dividend payment is not compulsory
  • No Repayment Burden: Capital need not be repaid
  • Creditworthiness: Increases borrowing capacity
  • Retained Control: No outsider interference (if promoters hold majority)
  • Risk Capital: Provides cushion against losses
  • No Charge on Assets: No security required
  • Democratic: Shareholders participate in decision making
  • Appreciation: Share value can increase over time

‚ùå Disadvantages of Equity Shares

  • Dilution of Control: More shares = control distributed
  • High Cost: Dividend expectations higher than interest
  • No Tax Benefit: Dividend paid from after-tax profits
  • Speculation: Share prices fluctuate in market
  • Over-capitalization Risk: If too many shares issued
  • Lengthy Process: SEBI regulations, complex issue procedure
  • Risk for Investors: No guaranteed returns
  • Market Dependency: Issue depends on market conditions

üìä Types of Equity Share Issues:

1. IPO (Initial Public Offer): First time company offers shares to public
2. FPO (Follow-on Public Offer): Already listed company issues more shares
3. Rights Issue: Offered to existing shareholders first
4. Bonus Issue: Free shares given to existing shareholders from reserves
5. Private Placement: Shares sold to selected investors, not public
6. ESOP: Employee Stock Option Plan - shares offered to employees

2. Preference Shares

üìñ Definition

Preference shares are shares that have preferential rights over equity shares in two matters:
1. Dividend Payment: Preference shareholders get dividend before equity shareholders
2. Capital Repayment: At winding up, preference shareholders are paid before equity shareholders

Key Features of Preference Shares:

  • ‚Ä¢ Fixed Dividend: Dividend rate is fixed (e.g., 8% preference shares)
  • ‚Ä¢ Priority in Dividend: Paid before equity dividend
  • ‚Ä¢ Priority in Repayment: Paid before equity at winding up
  • ‚Ä¢ Usually No Voting Rights: Can vote only on matters affecting them
  • ‚Ä¢ Hybrid Security: Features of both equity and debt
  • ‚Ä¢ Redeemable: In India, all preference shares must be redeemed within 20 years

Types of Preference Shares:

1Ô∏è‚É£ Cumulative vs Non-Cumulative

  • Cumulative: Unpaid dividend accumulates. If not paid this year, carries forward to next year. All arrears must be paid before equity dividend.
  • Non-Cumulative: Dividend does NOT accumulate. If not paid this year, that year's dividend is lost forever.

2Ô∏è‚É£ Convertible vs Non-Convertible

  • Convertible: Can be converted into equity shares after specified period. Holder gets option to become owner.
  • Non-Convertible: Cannot be converted to equity. Remains preference share till redemption.

3Ô∏è‚É£ Redeemable vs Irredeemable

  • Redeemable: Company can buy back after specified period. Has maturity date. (All preference shares in India must be redeemable within 20 years)
  • Irredeemable: Cannot be redeemed during company's lifetime. (Not allowed in India anymore)

4Ô∏è‚É£ Participating vs Non-Participating

  • Participating: After getting fixed dividend, also share in surplus profits along with equity shareholders.
  • Non-Participating: Get only fixed dividend, no share in extra profits.

‚úÖ Advantages of Preference Shares

  • No Voting Dilution: Control not affected
  • Fixed Dividend: Predictable cost
  • No Charge on Assets: No security needed
  • Flexible: Dividend can be skipped in tough times
  • Appeals to Conservative Investors: Less risky than equity
  • Redeemable: Can be paid off when company has funds

‚ùå Disadvantages of Preference Shares

  • Higher Cost than Debt: Dividend rate usually higher than interest
  • No Tax Benefit: Dividend not tax deductible
  • Cumulative Burden: Arrears keep piling up
  • Limited Appeal: Neither fully ownership nor fully debt
  • Skipping Dividend: Affects company's image

3. Retained Earnings (Ploughing Back of Profits)

üìñ Definition

Retained Earnings are the portion of profits that are NOT distributed as dividends but kept back (retained) in the business for future use. This is also called "Ploughing Back of Profits" or "Self-Financing" or "Internal Financing".

Formula: Retained Earnings = Net Profit - Dividends - Taxes - Transfers to Reserves

‚úÖ Advantages of Retained Earnings

  • Cheapest Source: No issue costs, no interest
  • No Fixed Obligation: Not mandatory to retain
  • No Dilution: Control remains same
  • No Repayment: Internal funds, no debt
  • Ready Availability: Already with company
  • No Formalities: No SEBI approval, no prospectus
  • Financial Strength: Shows company is profitable
  • Increases Creditworthiness: Improves borrowing capacity

‚ùå Disadvantages of Retained Earnings

  • Limited Amount: Depends on profits
  • Shareholder Dissatisfaction: Less dividend paid
  • Opportunity Cost: Shareholders could invest elsewhere
  • Misuse Possible: Management may waste funds
  • Only for Profitable Companies: Loss-making companies can't use
  • Share Price Impact: Low dividend = lower share price

üí° Factors Affecting Retained Earnings:

‚Ä¢ Profitability: Higher profits = more retention possible
‚Ä¢ Dividend Policy: Liberal dividend = less retention
‚Ä¢ Taxation: Higher taxes = less available for retention
‚Ä¢ Growth Plans: Expansion needs = more retention
‚Ä¢ Shareholder Expectations: If shareholders want dividend
‚Ä¢ Legal Requirements: Some profits must go to reserves

üè¶ Borrowed Funds (Debt Capital)

üìñ What are Borrowed Funds?

Borrowed Funds are funds raised through borrowing from external sources. These must be repaid after specified period along with interest. Lenders are creditors, not owners. They have no voting rights but have priority in repayment.

1. Debentures

üìñ Definition

A Debenture is a written acknowledgement of debt by a company. It is a certificate issued by company stating that it has borrowed money and promises to pay back with interest. Debenture holders are CREDITORS, not owners.

üîπ Fixed Interest
Debenture holders get fixed interest rate (e.g., 10% debentures) regardless of profits or losses.
üîπ No Voting Rights
Debenture holders cannot vote in company meetings. They are creditors, not owners.
üîπ Priority in Payment
Interest paid before dividend. At winding up, debenture holders paid before shareholders.
üîπ Redeemable
Must be repaid on maturity date specified at the time of issue.
üîπ May be Secured
Can be secured against company's assets (secured debentures) or unsecured (naked debentures).
üîπ Tax Deductible
Interest paid on debentures is TAX DEDUCTIBLE - reduces taxable income.

Types of Debentures:

üîê Based on Security

  • Secured/Mortgage Debentures: Backed by company's assets as collateral. If company fails to pay, assets can be sold.
  • Unsecured/Naked Debentures: No specific asset backing. Higher risk for lender, so higher interest rate.

üìù Based on Registration

  • Registered Debentures: Holder's name recorded in company register. Transfer requires formal procedure.
  • Bearer Debentures: Transferable by mere delivery. No name recorded. Like currency notes.

üîÑ Based on Convertibility

  • Convertible Debentures: Can be converted into equity shares after specified period.
    • Fully Convertible (FCD): Entire debenture converts
    • Partly Convertible (PCD): Part converts, part remains debenture
  • Non-Convertible Debentures (NCD): Cannot be converted. Pure debt instrument.

‚è±Ô∏è Based on Redemption

  • Redeemable Debentures: Repaid after specified period. Most common type.
  • Irredeemable/Perpetual Debentures: Not repaid during company's lifetime. Only interest paid. (Rare)

‚úÖ Advantages of Debentures

  • No Dilution of Control: Debenture holders can't vote
  • Tax Benefit: Interest is tax deductible
  • Fixed Cost: Interest rate is fixed, predictable
  • Trading on Equity: If ROI > Interest rate, benefits equity shareholders
  • Flexibility: Various types available
  • Investor Preference: Appeals to risk-averse investors

‚ùå Disadvantages of Debentures

  • Fixed Obligation: Interest must be paid even in loss
  • Repayment Pressure: Must repay on maturity
  • Charge on Assets: Secured debentures require security
  • Reduces Borrowing Capacity: Already leveraged
  • Risky During Recession: Fixed payments in tough times
  • Restrictions: Covenants may limit company's actions

2. Term Loans (from Banks & Financial Institutions)

üìñ Definition

Term Loans are loans for a fixed period taken from banks or financial institutions for medium to long-term needs. They are repaid in installments (EMI) over the loan period along with interest.

üìã Features of Term Loans

  • Fixed Period: Usually 3 to 10 years
  • Regular Installments: Monthly/quarterly EMI
  • Security Required: Usually secured by assets
  • Fixed/Floating Interest: Rate may be fixed or linked to base rate
  • Purpose-specific: For buying machinery, expansion, etc.
  • Processing Fee: Banks charge processing fee

üè¶ Major Lenders

  • Commercial Banks: SBI, HDFC Bank, ICICI Bank
  • Development Banks: IDBI, IFCI, SIDBI
  • NBFCs: Bajaj Finance, L&T Finance
  • Specialized Institutions: NABARD (agriculture), NHB (housing)

3. Public Deposits

üìñ Definition

Public Deposits are deposits accepted directly from the public by a company. Instead of going to banks, companies invite public to deposit money with them for fixed period at specified interest rate.

Features of Public Deposits:

  • ‚Ä¢ Period: Minimum 6 months, Maximum 36 months (3 years)
  • ‚Ä¢ Interest Rate: Usually higher than bank FD
  • ‚Ä¢ Unsecured: Not backed by any security
  • ‚Ä¢ Regulated by RBI: Companies (Acceptance of Deposits) Rules
  • ‚Ä¢ Limit: Cannot exceed 35% of paid-up capital + free reserves
  • ‚Ä¢ Credit Rating: Required for accepting deposits

‚úÖ Advantages

  • Simple procedure - no SEBI
  • No dilution of control
  • Lower cost than shares
  • No security required
  • Tax deductible interest

‚ùå Disadvantages

  • Unsecured - risky for depositors
  • Limited period (max 3 years)
  • Regulatory restrictions
  • Affects creditworthiness
  • Repayment obligation

‚è±Ô∏è Short-term Sources of Finance

üìñ What are Short-term Sources?

Short-term sources are funds required for less than one year to meet working capital needs - daily operations, buying raw materials, paying salaries, etc.

üè™ 1. Trade Credit

  • Meaning: Credit extended by suppliers for purchase of goods
  • Period: Usually 30 to 90 days
  • Cost: Generally interest-free
  • Based on: Trust, relationship, past dealings
  • Example: Buying raw materials on 60 days credit
  • Advantage: Easy, no formalities, no interest
  • Disadvantage: May lose cash discount, limited amount

üè¶ 2. Bank Overdraft

  • Meaning: Permission to withdraw more than account balance
  • Account: Linked to current account
  • Limit: Pre-approved maximum limit
  • Interest: Only on amount actually overdrawn
  • Security: May or may not be required
  • Advantage: Flexible, interest on usage only
  • Disadvantage: Bank can cancel anytime, higher interest

üíµ 3. Cash Credit

  • Meaning: Loan facility against security of stock/debtors
  • Security: Hypothecation of inventory, receivables
  • Limit: Based on value of security (drawing power)
  • Interest: On amount used, not on limit
  • Difference from OD: Cash credit requires security
  • Advantage: Larger limit, interest on usage
  • Disadvantage: Security required, documentation

üìÑ 4. Commercial Paper (CP)

  • Meaning: Unsecured promissory note issued by large corporates
  • Maturity: 15 days to 1 year (usually 90-180 days)
  • Minimum Amount: ‚Çπ5 lakhs (multiples of ‚Çπ5 lakh)
  • Issued by: Highly rated companies (minimum A3 rating)
  • Sold at: Discount to face value
  • Regulated by: RBI
  • Advantage: Lower cost than bank loan, prestigious
  • Disadvantage: Only for big companies with good credit

üìä 5. Factoring

  • Meaning: Selling accounts receivable (debtors) to a factor at discount
  • Factor: Bank or specialized NBFC
  • Process: Sell invoices ‚Üí Get 80-90% immediately ‚Üí Balance after collection
  • Advantage: Immediate cash, no collection hassle
  • Disadvantage: Costly (factor charges fee/discount)

üí≥ 6. Bank Loans (Short-term)

  • Meaning: Loans from banks for less than 1 year
  • Types: Demand loan, Short-term loan
  • Security: Usually required
  • Interest: Fixed or floating rate
  • Purpose: Working capital, seasonal needs

üìú 7. Discounting of Bills

  • Meaning: Bank buys bill of exchange before maturity at discount
  • Process: Seller has bill ‚Üí Bank pays (face value - discount) ‚Üí Bank collects from buyer on maturity
  • Advantage: Immediate cash for seller
  • Disadvantage: Discount reduces amount received

üÜï Special/Modern Sources of Finance

üè≠ 1. Lease Financing

  • Meaning: Using an asset without buying it - pay rent instead
  • Lessor: Owner of asset (gives on lease)
  • Lessee: User of asset (takes on lease)
  • Ownership: Remains with lessor
  • Types:
    • Operating Lease: Short-term, cancellable, lessor maintains
    • Finance Lease: Long-term, non-cancellable, lessee maintains
  • Advantage: No large capital outflow, tax benefits
  • Example: Leasing office space, machinery, vehicles

üöó 2. Hire Purchase

  • Meaning: Buying asset in installments over time
  • Process: Down payment + Monthly installments (EMI)
  • Ownership: Transfers to buyer after LAST installment
  • Possession: Buyer gets immediate possession
  • Default: Seller can repossess if installments not paid
  • Common for: Cars, two-wheelers, consumer durables
  • Difference from Lease: Ownership eventually transfers in HP

üíº 3. Venture Capital

  • Meaning: Investment in new, risky, high-potential ventures
  • Investors: Venture Capital Funds (VCFs)
  • Nature: Equity participation in startups
  • Risk: Very high (many startups fail)
  • Return Expected: Very high (10x-100x if successful)
  • Exit: Through IPO or sale to another company
  • Example: Sequoia, Accel investing in Flipkart, Zomato
  • Also provides: Mentorship, network, guidance

üìä 4. GDR/ADR (Global/American Depository Receipts)

  • Purpose: Raise funds from foreign markets
  • GDR: Global Depository Receipts
    • Issued in international markets (Europe, Asia)
    • Listed on foreign exchanges (London, Luxembourg)
    • Denominated in foreign currency (usually USD)
  • ADR: American Depository Receipts
    • Issued specifically in US markets
    • Listed on NYSE, NASDAQ
    • Denominated in US Dollars
  • Indian companies with ADRs: Infosys, HDFC Bank, ICICI Bank
