What is a Cash Book?

Definition

A Cash Book is a special journal or book of original entry in which all cash transactions (both cash receipts and cash payments) are recorded in chronological order. It serves a dual purpose â€“ it functions both as a journal (book of original entry) and as a ledger account (Cash Account).

The Cash Book is maintained to keep a systematic record of all cash and bank transactions of a business. Unlike other subsidiary books that record only one type of transaction, the Cash Book records both the debit side (receipts) and credit side (payments) of cash transactions. The balance of the Cash Book represents the cash in hand or cash at bank at any given point in time.

Key Point

The Cash Book is unique because it eliminates the need to maintain a separate Cash Account in the ledger. It acts as both the primary book of entry for cash transactions and the ledger account for cash.

Advantages of Cash Book

Maintaining a Cash Book offers several significant benefits for businesses of all sizes:

Dual Function
The Cash Book serves both as a journal and a ledger, eliminating the need to post cash entries to a separate cash account in the ledger. This saves time and reduces duplication of work in bookkeeping.
Complete Record
All cash receipts and payments are recorded in one place, providing a comprehensive view of all cash movements. This makes it easy to track and monitor cash flow at any point in time.
Easy Detection of Errors
Since the Cash Book is balanced regularly (daily, weekly, or monthly), any discrepancies or errors in cash transactions can be detected and corrected immediately, ensuring accuracy in accounts.
Accurate Cash Position
The Cash Book provides an instant and accurate picture of the cash position of the business. The balance shows exactly how much cash is available, helping in better cash management and decision-making.

Difference Between Cash Account and Cash Book

While both relate to cash transactions, there are important distinctions between a Cash Account and a Cash Book:

Basis Cash Account Cash Book
Nature It is a ledger account It is both a journal and a ledger
Recording Cash transactions are first recorded in the journal, then posted to the Cash Account Cash transactions are directly recorded in the Cash Book (no separate journal entry needed)
Format Simple T-format with debit and credit sides More detailed format with columns for date, particulars, V.No., L.F., and amount
Narration Generally does not contain detailed narration Contains brief narration/description for each transaction
Balancing Balanced periodically (monthly or as needed) Usually balanced daily or very frequently
Types Only one type Multiple types: Single Column, Double Column, Petty Cash Book, etc.

Types of Cash Book

Based on the nature and volume of transactions, Cash Books are classified into three main types:

Single Column Cash Book

This is the simplest form of Cash Book with only one amount column on each side (debit and credit). It records only cash transactions and is suitable for small businesses with limited transactions.

When to Use:

  • When the business deals only in cash (no bank transactions)
  • When there are no discount transactions
  • Suitable for small retail shops, vendors, and petty businesses

This Cash Book has two amount columns on each side â€“ one for cash and one for discount. It records both cash transactions and discount allowed (on receipts side) and discount received (on payments side).

When to Use:

  • When the business offers cash discounts to customers (discount allowed)
  • When the business receives cash discounts from suppliers (discount received)
  • Suitable for businesses with regular credit sales and purchases

Petty Cash Book

A Petty Cash Book is maintained to record small, routine expenses of the business. It works on the Imprest System, where a fixed amount is allocated to the petty cashier at the beginning of a period, and it is replenished periodically.

When to Use:

  • For recording small daily expenses like tea, stationery, postage, taxi fares, etc.
  • When the business wants to reduce the burden on the main cashier
  • Helps in better control and monitoring of small expenses

Imprest System:

Under the Imprest System, a fixed amount (say â‚¹5,000) is given to the petty cashier. At the end of the period, the amount spent is reimbursed, bringing the petty cash back to the original imprest amount.

Types of Entries in Cash Book

Cash Book entries can be broadly classified into the following categories:

Cash Receipts
All transactions where cash is received are recorded on the debit (left) side. Examples: Cash sales, cash received from debtors, capital introduced, loans received, etc. Entry format: "To [Account Name]"
Cash Payments
All transactions where cash is paid are recorded on the credit (right) side. Examples: Cash purchases, cash paid to creditors, expenses paid, drawings, etc. Entry format: "By [Account Name]"
Contra Entries
Transactions where money is transferred from cash to bank or bank to cash. These entries appear on both sides with "C" in the L.F. column. They don't affect the total cash and bank combined.
Opening & Closing Balances
Opening balance (Balance b/d) appears on the debit side at the beginning. Closing balance (Balance c/d) appears on the credit side to balance the cash book. Next period starts with Balance b/d on debit side.

Golden Rules for Cash Book Entries:

  • Debit Side (Receipts): All receipts use "To" before the account name
  • Credit Side (Payments): All payments use "By" before the account name
  • Balancing: Cash book can never have a credit balance (you can't pay more cash than you have)
  • Narration: Brief description is written below each entry for clarity
  • Contra Entry: Marked with "C" in the L.F. column on both sides

Important Points to Remember

Cash Book Never Has Credit Balance
Unlike other ledger accounts, a Cash Book can never show a credit balance because you cannot pay more cash than you have. If calculations show a credit balance, there's an error that needs correction.
Contra Entries
Transactions between cash and bank (deposits and withdrawals) are called contra entries. They appear on both sides and are marked with "C" in the L.F. column. They don't affect total liquid assets.
Discount Columns
In Double/Three Column Cash Books, discount columns are only totaled, never balanced. Discount Allowed is posted to the debit side of Discount Allowed A/c, and Discount Received to the credit side of Discount Received A/c.
Regular Balancing
Cash Books should be balanced frequently (daily or weekly) to detect errors quickly and maintain accurate cash position. This helps in better cash management and prevents fraud.
